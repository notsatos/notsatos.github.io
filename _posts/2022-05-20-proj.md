If we're given two curves, and we wanted to study all the points of intersection of the given curves, then there is a possibility that these curves do not intersect. Namely, when the curves are parallel to one another. The idea behind projective geometryô is that we want to enlarge the underlying plane we're working with (an affine space) to have an intersection "at infinity" where the curves intersect, given that they didn't already intersect in the original plane (this little stipulation is important). We want there to be at least one intersection between two curves, always.ô 

A way to attach these points at infinity is to embed the affine space $\aff^n$ into the vector space $k^{n+1}$ by identifying to each point $(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) \in \aff^n$ with the newô  point $( 1, x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) \in k^{n+1}$; that is, we're embedding $\aff^n \to k^{n+1}$ by the map $(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) \longmapsto ( 1, x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$ and then considering the $1$-dimensional linear subspace in $k^{n+1}$ spanned by this vector. Note that every point $(1 , x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$ determines a line in $k^{n+1}$ through the origin of $k^{n+1}$. The fact that we're working over a vector space here is important as this is a valid thing to do since if $v$ is a vector, we can define a line as the set of allô  $\lambda v$ for all $\lambda \in k$, and the line passes through the origin as $\lambda = 0 \in k$. Additionally, every line through the origin of $k^{n+1}$, except for those lying in the plane $x_0 = 0$, corresponds to a unique point of the form $(1,ô  x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$. And the lines through the origin of $k^{n+1}$, in the plane $x_0 = 0$, can be thought of as corresponding to the points at infinity. As an example, if we take a point $(c_1, c_2) \in \aff^2$, this produces a point $(1, c_1, c_2) \in k^3$ and a corresponding line through the origin $(0, 0, 0)$. 

We define the projective plane, which is denoted $\pp^1 (\aff^2) = \pp (\aff^2)$, as the set of all such $1$-dimensional linear subspaces of $k^3$. This is equivalent to saying that the projective plane is the collection of all lines through the origin coming from points of $\aff^2$, with the addition of including lines contained in the plane where $x_0 = 0$, like $Q$ in the image above, that don't arise in this manner. Lines that have the qualities of $Q$ can be though of as limits of lines coming from an unbounded sequence of points in $\aff^2$ (as displayed by in the image on the right above). These are to be interpreted as the points at infinity that we were seeking. To reiterate the image to the right above, as it's the most insightful I believe, if we are considering a line $L$ through the origin of $k^3$, which doesn't lie on the plane $x_0 = 0$, then the $L$ intersects only once with $(1, x_1, x_2) (=H_1)$, the hyperplaneô cut out by $x_0 = 1$. Now imagine that we are varying the line $L$ to begin to rotate towards the plane $x_0 = 0$ (as depicted in the second image by the line $Q$), so that we ultimately get it to lie in the plane $x_0 = 0$. Then the point of intersection with $H_1$ will then go off to infinityô (look at the lines stretching farthest farthest to the right of in the second image) and disappears as the varying line $L$ comes to rest in the plane $x_0 = 0$. The limiting point, which determines the line $L$, is our point of intersection! The point at infinity!

Definition 1. Let $k$ be a field. The Projective $n$-space over $k$, written $\pp^n (k)$, or just $\pp^n$ when context is clear, is defined to be the set of all lines through $(0, 0,\ldots, 0)$ in $k^{n+1}$.ô 

Moreover, any point $(x) = (x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n) \neq (0, 0, \ldots, 0)$ determines a unique such that line that passes through the origin: the line is given by the set $\{ \lambda x_0, \lambda x_1, \ldots, \lambda x_n \colon \lambda \in k \}$. Two points $(x)$ and $(y)$ produce the same line if and only if there exists some nonzero $\lambda \in k$ such that $(x_0, \ldots, x_n) = (\lambda y_0, \ldots, \lambda y_n)$ãif this is the case, we say that $(x)$ and $(y)$ are equivalent. Then $\pp^n (k)$ can be established byô 

$$ \pp^n (k) = (k^{n+1} \setminus (0, \ldots, 0) )/ \sim$$

with the equivalence relation $(x_0, \ldots, x_n) \sim (y_0, \ldots, y_n)$ if and only if $(x_0, \ldots, x_n) = (\lambda y_0, \ldots, \lambda y_n)$ for some $\lambda \in k\setminus \{0 \}$. Equivalence classes of $(x_0, \ldots, x_n)$ is usually denoted by $(x_0 \colon \cdots \colon x_n) \in \pp^n (k)$. We call $x_0, \ldots, x_n$ the homogeneous or projective coordinatesô of the points $(x_0 \colon \cdots x_n )$.

As our initial construction of projective space went along, we can now say that we embed the affine space $\aff^n$ into $\pp^n (k)$ by the map $(x_0, \ldots, x_n ) \mapsto (1\colon x_1 \colon \cdots \colon x_n )$ whose imageô is defined to be the subset $U_0 = \{(x_0 \colon \cdots \colon x_n ) \colon x_0 \neq 0 \}$ of $\pp^n (k)$. In this way, by setting $x_0 = 1$, we can describe $ \aff^n$ as being a subset of $\pp^n (k)$. We call coordinates $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ the inhomogenous or affine coordinates on $U_0$. And the remaining points of $\pp^n (k)$ are of the form $(0 \colon x_1 \colon \cdots \colon x_n)$ (some authors identify this as being the set $H_{\infty} := \{ (x_0 \colon \cdots \colon x_n ) \in \pp^n (k) \colon x_0 = 0 \}$). We "forget" the coordinate $x_0 = 0$ (as it's zero) and $H_\infty$ is naturally bijective to $\pp^{n-1} (k)$ãas it corresponds to the $1$-dimensional linear subspaces of $k^n$. Points in the set $H_\infty$ are said to be points at infinity, and so there is one such point in every direction in $k^n$. Additionally, $H_\infty$ is said to be the hyperplane at infinity.

Now $\pp^0 (k)$ consists of a single point (easiest to think of $\pp^0$ as being all the lines which go through the origin of $k^1 = k$, which we identify as being the "point at infinity", which we denote as $\{ \infty \} = \pp^0 (k)$. So then we write $\pp^1 (k) = \aff^1 \cup \{ \infty \}$. Moreover, using a stereographic projection, we can understand $\pp^1 (k)$ as the set of lines through a fixed point: 

Recall that a line is described by $ax+by + c = 0$ where both $a, b$ are not zero. In looking at the projective plane, our attention is aimed at $(a \colon b)$. To emphasize $\pp^1$, if we consider $\pp^1 (\rr)$, we can notice that if $(x \colon y) \in \pp^1 (\rr)$ and $y \neq 0$, then $(x, y) \sim (x/y, 1)$ as we $\lambda = 1/y$, and so the class $(x \colon y)$ contains a unique representative $(\ell, 1)$ where $\ell = x/y \in \rr$. So then we can think of $\pp^1 (\rr)$ as the following:

$$\pp^1 (\rr) = \{ (x \colon 1 ) \colon x \in \rr \} \cup \{(1 \colon 0) \}$$

where the points $\{( x \colon 1 ) \}$ form a real line and $(1 \colon 0 )$ is called the point at infinity. 

We can carry this on for greater dimensions of $\pp (\rr)$: If $x_0 \neq 0$, then $(x_0 , x_1, x_2) \sim (1, \frac{x_1}{x_0}, \frac{x_2}{x_0} )$, and soô 

$$\pp^2 (\rr) = \{ (1 \colon x_1 \colon x_2) \colon x_1,x_2 \in \rr \} \cup \{ (0 \colon a \colon b) \colon a,b \in \rr \}.$$

And so the points of $ \{ (1 \colon x_1 \colon x_2) \colon x_1,y \in \rr \}$ are in $1$-to-$1$ correspondence with the real plane, and the points in $\{ (0 \colon a \colon b) \colon a,b \in \rr \}$ are the points at infinity and can be identified with $\pp^1(\rr)$.ô 

Proposition 1. Parallel lines in the real plane intersect at a point at infinity.ô 

Proof. Let $L \colon y = mx + b$ and $L^\prime \colon y =mx +b^\prime$ define parallel lines that are unique in $\rr^2$. Now, if we take points $(k_1, k_2) \in \rr^2$, then this corresponds to lines $(k_1 \colon k_2) \in \pp^1 (\rr)$, and so, in analogy, lines in $\rr^2$ correspond to planes in $\pp^2 (\rr)$:ô 

\begin{align} L &= \{ (x \colon y \colon z) \colon mx-bz -y =0 \}, \\ L^\prime &= \{ ( x \colon y \colon z ) \colon mx-b^\prime z -y = 0 \} .\end{align} Now as we want to find a common point we take the intersection of $L$ and $L^\prime$. A point of intersection $(x \colon y \colon z) \in L \cap L^\prime$ satisfies $mx+bz-y = mx+b^\prime -y $, which ultimately implies $z(b^\prime - b) =0$. If $b^\prime = b$, then this gives $L = L^\prime$ which contradicts the fact that they're parallel; thus we must have $z=0$. Hence

\begin{align}Lô  \cap L^\prime &= \{ (x \colon y \colon 0 ) \colon mx-y=0 \}ô  \\ &= \{ (x \colon mx \colon 0) \colon x \in \rr \} \\ &= \{ (1 \colon m \colon 0) \}, \end{align} and this is a point at infinityãa single point at infinity. 

Now as we have done before, we were able to describe $\pp^1(\rr) \simeq \mathbb S^1$, and the title of this post suggests, we can do the same for $\pp^1 (\cc)$, but this one is a bit cooler in my opinion.ô 

If we let $k = \cc$, then we claim that $\pp^n$ is a compact space (with the classical topology, not Zariski). Let $$S^{2n+1} = \{(a_0, \ldots, a_n) \in \cc^{n+1} \colon |a_0|^2 + \cdots + |a_n|^2 = 1 \}$$

be the unit sphere in $\cc^{n+1} = \rr^{2n+2}$. So this space is compact as it's closed and bounded. Now there is a surjective map $$ S^{2n+1} \to \pp^n (\cc), (a_0, \ldots, a_n) \longmapsto (a_0 \colon \cdots \colon a_n)$$

which is clearly surjective, and so images of compact sets are compact under continuous maps, then $\pp^n (k)$ is also compact. By what we established in Example 1, $\pp^1 (\cc) = \cc^1 \cup \{\infty \}$. So it is topologically a sphere, and we can visualize it in $\rr^3,$ and it is indeed the Riemann sphere $\widehat{\cc}$

Ultimately we hope to do algebraic geometry over this new geometry we've just constructed, and in fact, most algebraic geometry is done over projective spaces, so we'll proceed in the rest of this post establishing how can start to do it.

Let $A = k[x_0, \ldots , x_n]$. If we take $f \in A$ and $P = (a_0 \colon \cdots \colon a_n) \in \pp^n$, then $f(P)$ doesn't make sense. For example, $f(x_0, x_1) =x^2_1 - x_0$ then $f(1, 1) =0$ but $f(-1, -1) \neq 0$, even though $(1 \colon 1)$ and $(-1 \colon -1)$ are the same point in $\pp^n$. We need to adapt your approach, and we do this by requiring a polynomial $f \in A$ of degree $d$ to be homogeneous, that is, $$f(\lambda x_0, \ldots, \lambda x_n) = \lambda^d f(x_0, \ldots, x_n) \text{ for all $\lambda \in k^\ast$},$$ which implies that $(\lambda x_0, \ldots, \lambda x_n) = 0 $ if and only if $f(x_0, \ldots, x_n) =0$, and so this makes sense when $f(P) = 0$ or $f(P) \neq 0$.ô 

Definition 2. Let $T \subseteq A$ be a set of homogeneous polynomials. Let $$V(T) = \{ P \in \pp^n \colon f(P) = 0 \text{ for all $f \in T$} \}.$$

For a homogeneous polynomial $f \in A$, we define $D(f) := \pp^n \setminus V(f)$. As we should expect, the definitions on $V$ give rise to the Zariski topology on $\pp^n$.ô 

Definition 3. A projective algebraic variety is a subset of $\pp^n$ of the form $V(T)$, together with the induced Zariski topology.ô 

To be clear, we have the following Zariski topology on (algebraic) projective varieties:

Lemma 1. Projective Varieties satisfy the folowing axioms of the closed sets of a topology on $\pp^n_k$; that tis, we have the following:

The union of finitely many projective varieties is a projective variety.
The intersection of any number of projective varieties is a projective variety
The empty set and $\pp^n_k$ are projective varieties.ô

Definition 4. A graded ring is a ring $S$ together with a decomposition into abelian groups $$S = \bigoplus_{d\geq 0} S_d$$ such that

for all $d \neq e$, we have $S_d \cap S_e = \{ 0 \},$ and
$S_d \cdot S_e \subset S_{d+e}$.
The elements of $S_d$ are called the homogeneous elements of degree $d$.

Example 2.ô An important example that we'll use is that we can define $$k^d [x_0, \ldots, x_n] := \{ f \in k[x_0, \ldots, x_n] \colon f \text{ is homogeneous of degree $d$} \} \cup \{0 \} $$ to grade $k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]$ in the following way:

$$k[x_0, \ldots, x_n] = \bigoplus_{d \geq 0} k^d [x_0, \ldots, x_n ].$$ So, an element $f \in S_d$ isô homogeneousô of degreeô d. Furthermore, an ideal $J$ in $S$ is called homogeneousô if it is generated by homogeneous elements; that is,

$$J = \bigoplus_{d \geq 0} (J \cap S_d).$$

so an ideal $J$ is homogeneous if and only if every element $f \in J$ has a unique composition $f = f_0 +\cdots +f_N,$ where each $f_i \in J$ is a homogeneous of degree $d_i$. And a homogeneous ideal $I$ is called primeô if and only if for any pair of homgeneous elemements $f, g$ in a graded ring $S$, we have $fg \in I$ if and only $f \in I$ or $g \in I$.ô

Definition 5. For a graded ring $S$, the irrelevant ideal of $S$ is $$S_{+} = \bigoplus_{d >0} S_d, $$ and $$\text{Proj} (S) = \{ \mathfrak{p} \trianglelefteq S \colon \mathfrak{p} \text{ is prime}, \text{ and } S_{+} \not \subseteq \mathfrak{p} \}.$$

So for a homogeneous ideal $J$, and a homogeneous $f \in A$, we let $V_+ (I) = \{ \mathfrak{p} \in \proj (S) \colon I \subseteq \mathfrak{p} \}$ and $D_+ (f) = \proj (S) \setminus V_+ (f)$, and so, as may expect, $V_+$ makes $\proj (S)$ into a topological space--just as we intended as wanted this to be analogous to $\spec$. For the person that already knows some scheme theory, we will eventually endow $\proj (S)$ with a sheaf of rings $\mathcal O _{\proj (S)}$ such that $(\proj (S), \mathcal O_{\proj (S)})$ is a scheme--and this will be explored later in a post related to schemes.ô
