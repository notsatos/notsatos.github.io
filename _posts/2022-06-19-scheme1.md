Classical algebraic geometry's central focus is the study of algebraic varieties, where spaces are locally described as being loci of sets of polynomial equations over an algebraically closed field—much work is typically done over $\cc$. From the previous post, we can define an affine algebraic $k$-variety (or simply affine variety) as an irreducible Zariski-closed subset of $k^n$ for an algebraically closed field $k$, and a quasi-affine variety is an open subset of an affine algebraic variety. Although these definitions provide us with the foundations of classical algebraic geometry, they can in fact be generalized in the language of schemes. The theory of schemes is done in the context of the spectrum of a ring, say, $A$, and it has been noted that it was André Martineau who initially suggested to Serre to use $\spec A$ as a new way of doing algebraic geometry. And an affine variety $V(f_1, \ldots f_r)$ of $k^n$ can be recovered from the affine scheme $\spec k [T_1, \ldots, T_n] / (f_1, \ldots, f_r)$.

A great benefit of working over with schemes (although we haven't defined them yet, just think of $\spec A$ with the Zariski topology) is that we're working over something that need not be an algebraically closed field, which is something we need to with varieties. Additionally, the theory of schemes provides us with allowing nilpotents—something that's not allowed in the classical setting. If we were to consider the equation $f(x) = x $ and $g(x) = x^2$, then $V(f) = V(g)$ as $x = 0$ and $x^2 = 0$ provide the same solution set. The variety here is blind to the multiplicity of the zeros, yet the variety will give rise to different schemes as we compare $k[x]/(x) \simeq k$ and $k[x] /(x^2)$—we will look at this particular example later on. 

For some generality, an affine $k$-variety is $\spec A$ for some finitely generated $k$-algebra $A$, but this can be further generalized: A $k$-variety is a scheme of finite type over the field $k$ (we'll begin to look further into this later on). For a refresher on some algebra terminology see the Appendix of this post. 

Now, a great discussion was given by Eisenbud in his The Geometry of Schemes on pg. $8$. To paraphrase him: given a ring $A$, the affine scheme $\spec A$ "models and generalizes the relationships between an affine variety and its coordinate ring". ## 1. Schemes 
Throughout this section, let $A$ denote a commutative ring with identity. From the post You Get a Topology, You Get a Topology, Everyone Gets a Topology (YGT), we gave the set $\spec A$ a topology, namely the Zariski topology. To proceed into getting into what a scheme is, we need to define a central part of it, which will make use of the Zariski topology. 

**Definition 1.1.** A ringed space $(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$ consists of a topological space $X$ and a sheaf of rings rings $\mathcal{O}_X$ on $X$, called the structure sheaf.

Recall from YGT that this means that for our topological $X$, we have an assignment of a ring $\mathcal{O}_X (U)$ to each open set $U$ in $X$ and $\oh_X$ satisfies the sheaf axioms. Now to extend some of Definition 1.1, a locally ringed space is a ringed space $(X, \oh_X)$ such that every stalk $\oh _{X, x}$ is a local ring, where $x \in X$. Once again, we should remind ourselves that a local ring is a ring $A$ such that it has a unique (!) maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}$, and we say that the field $A/ \mathfrak{m}$ is the residue field of $A$ (we usually write $\kappa (\mathfrak{m}) = A/\mathfrak{m}$ to denote the residue field). This is an important point as later on we'll see how we actually go about working with the structure sheaf $\oh_X$ and producing local rings for each open set $U$ of $X$. 

**Lemma 1.1.** Let $A$ be a ring and $\mathfrak{m}$ be a proper ideal of $A$. The following are equivalent:   
(1)$\; \;$ $A$ is a local ring with maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}$,   
(2)$\; \;$ $\spec A$ has exactly one closed point,    
(3)$\; \;$ $\mathfrak{m}$ is maximal and all elements in $1+\mathfrak{m}$ are invertible, and   
(4)$\; \;$ All elements in $A \setminus  \mathfrak{m}$ are invertible. 

Example 1.1. The localization of $A$ at some prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}$, $A _\mathfrak{p}$, is a local ring with a unique maximal ideal $\mathfrak{p} A _{\mathfrak{p}}$. The reason this is true is as follows: Consider the ideal $\mathfrak p A _\mathfrak{p}:=\mathfrak{m} _{\mathfrak{p}}$. Then we have elements of the form $\frac{p}{1} \cdot \frac{a}{b} = \frac{ap}{b} \in \mathfrak{m} _{\mathfrak{p}}$, where $p \in \mathfrak p$, $a \in A$, and $b \in A\setminus \mathfrak{p}$. Thus we have that $\mathfrak{m} _{\mathfrak{p}} = \{ \frac{x}{y} \colon x \in \mathfrak p, y \in A \setminus \mathfrak p \}$. Note that all elements in $\mathfrak{m} _{\mathfrak{p}}$ are non-units. Now all elements in $A _{\mathfrak{p}} \setminus \mathfrak{m} _{\mathfrak{p}}$ are of the form $\frac{s}{t}$ where $s \notin \mathfrak p$ and $t \in A _p \setminus \mathfrak p$, Recall the fact that $\frac{a}{b} \in A _\mathfrak p $ is a unit if and only if $a \in A \setminus \mathfrak p$. So then we have that $\frac{s}{t}$ is a unit and thus by (4) in Lemma 1.1, we have that $A _\mathfrak p$ is a local ring with unique maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m} _{\mathfrak{p}}$.
