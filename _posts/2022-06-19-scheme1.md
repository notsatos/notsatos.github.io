Classical algebraic geometry's central focus is the study of algebraic varieties, where spaces are locally described as being loci of sets of polynomial equations over an algebraically closed field—much work is typically done over $\cc$. From the previous post, we can define an affine algebraic $k$-variety (or simply affine variety) as an irreducible Zariski-closed subset of $k^n$ for an algebraically closed field $k$, and a quasi-affine variety is an open subset of an affine algebraic variety. Although these definitions provide us with the foundations of classical algebraic geometry, they can in fact be generalized in the language of schemes. The theory of schemes is done in the context of the spectrum of a ring, say, $A$, and it has been noted that it was André Martineau who initially suggested to Serre to use $\spec A$ as a new way of doing algebraic geometry. Furthermore, in this context, an affine variety $V(f_1, \ldots f_r)$ of $k^n$ can be recovered from the affine scheme $\spec k [T_1, \ldots, T_n] / (f_1, \ldots, f_r)$.

A great benefit of working over with schemes (although we haven't defined them yet, just think of $\spec A$ with the Zariski topology) is that we're working over something that need not be an algebraically closed field, which is something we need to with varieties. Additionally, the theory of schemes provides us with allowing nilpotents—something that's not allowed in the classical setting. If we were to consider the equation $f(x) = x $ and $g(x) = x^2$, then $V(f) = V(g)$ as $x = 0$ and $x^2 = 0$ provide the same solution set. The variety here is blind to the multiplicity of the zeros, yet the variety will give rise to different schemes as we compare $k[x]/(x) \simeq k$ and $k[x] /(x^2)$—we will look at this particular example later on. 

For some generality, an affine $k$-variety is $\spec A$ for some finitely generated $k$-algebra $A$, but this can be further generalized: A $k$-variety is a scheme of finite type over the field $k$ (we'll begin to look further into this later on). For a refresher on some algebra terminology see the Appendix of this post. 

Now, a great discussion was given by Eisenbud in his The Geometry of Schemes on pg. $8$. To paraphrase him: given a ring $A$, the affine scheme $\spec A$ "models and generalizes the relationships between an affine variety and its coordinate ring". ## 1. Endowing $\spec A$ with a topology. 
Wwe define the *Vanishing set*, for any ideal $\mathfrak{a}$ of a ring $A$, to be the subset $V(\mathfrak{a}) \subseteq \spec A$ given by \\[ V(\mathfrak{a}) := \\{ \p \in \spec A \colon \mathfrak{a} \subseteq \mathfrak{p}. \\} \\] This is, equivalently, the set of points on which all elements of $\mathfrak{a}$ are zero, that is, $x \in V(\mathfrak a)$ if $x \in \spec A$ and $f(x) = 0$ for all $f \in \mathfrak {a}$. Now, understanding this last point where $f$ is a *function* of $\mathfrak{a}$ is established on the fact if $\mathfrak p \in \spec A$, then the localization $A _{\mathfrak p}$ is a local ring (see Lemma 1.1 below) with a maximal ideal $\mathfrak p A _{\mathfrak p} :=\mathfrak{m} _{\mathfrak p}$. The reason being is that we can define a so-called *residue field* $\kappa (\mathfrak p):=A _{\mathfrak p}/ \mathfrak{m} _{\mathfrak p}$ where a function/element $f \in \mathfrak{a}$ reduces modulo $\mathfrak p$ produces an element in $f (\mathfrak{p}) \in \kappa (\mathfrak p)$. On this last point, the value at the point $\mathfrak p$, which we will soon regard as just a point of $\spec A$, is just $f \Mod{ \mathfrak p}$. For example, the function $5 \in \mathfrak a = (5)$ of $ \spec \zz$ takes the value $1 \Mod{ 2}$ at the prime ideal $(2)$, $2 \Mod{3}$ at $(3)$, and $0 \Mod{5}$ at $(5)$---the point vanishes at the *point* $(5)$. Furthermore, an element $f$ of the ring lying in a prime ideal $\mathfrak p$ is equivalent to saying that the function vanishes at the point $\mathfrak p$; that is, $f(\mathfrak p) = 0$ if and only if $f \in \mathfrak p$.


**Lemma 1.1.** Let $A$ be a ring and $\mathfrak{m}$ be a proper ideal of $A$. The following are equivalent:   
(1)$\; \;$ $A$ is a local ring with maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}$,   
(2)$\; \;$ $\spec A$ has exactly one closed point,    
(3)$\; \;$ $\mathfrak{m}$ is maximal and all elements in $1+\mathfrak{m}$ are invertible, and   
(4)$\; \;$ All elements in $A \setminus  \mathfrak{m}$ are invertible. 

**Example 1.1.**The localization of $A$ at some prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}$, $A _\mathfrak{p}$, is a local ring with a unique maximal ideal $\mathfrak{p} A _{\mathfrak{p}}$. The reason this is true is as follows: Consider the ideal $\mathfrak p A _\mathfrak{p}:=\mathfrak{m} _{\mathfrak{p}}$. Then we have elements of the form $\frac{p}{1} \cdot \frac{a}{b} = \frac{ap}{b} \in \mathfrak{m} _{\mathfrak{p}}$, where $p \in \mathfrak p$, $a \in A$, and $b \in A\setminus \mathfrak{p}$. Thus we have that $\mathfrak{m} _{\mathfrak{p}} = \{ \frac{x}{y} \colon x \in \mathfrak p, y \in A \setminus \mathfrak p \}$. Note that all elements in $\mathfrak{m} _{\mathfrak{p}}$ are non-units. Now all elements in $A _{\mathfrak{p}} \setminus \mathfrak{m} _{\mathfrak{p}}$ are of the form $\frac{s}{t}$ where $s \notin \mathfrak p$ and $t \in A _p \setminus \mathfrak p$, Recall the fact that $\frac{a}{b} \in A _\mathfrak p $ is a unit if and only if $a \in A \setminus \mathfrak p$. So then we have that $\frac{s}{t}$ is a unit and thus by (4) in Lemma 1.1, we have that $A _\mathfrak p$ is a local ring with unique maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m} _{\mathfrak{p}}$. 
 
Now we want to proceed with endowing $\spec A$ with a topology, which we mostly do so in the following Lemma. 

**Lemma 1.2.**    
(a) If $\mathfrak a$ and $\mathfrak b$ are two ideals of $A$ then $V(\mathfrak {ab}) = V (\mathfrak{a}) \cup V( \mathfrak b)$.     
(b) If $\\{ \mathfrak a _i \\}$ is a family of ideals of $A$, then $V\left ( \sum \mathfrak a _i \right) = \bigcap V( \mathfrak a _i)$.    
(c) If $\mathfrak{a}$ and $\mathfrak b$ are two ideals, then $V(\mathfrak a) \subseteq V (\mathfrak b)$ if and only if $\sqrt{\mathfrak b} \subseteq \sqrt{\mathfrak a}$. 

*Proof.* (a) Suppose $ \mathfrak{ab} \subseteq \mathfrak{p}$. If $\mathfrak{a} \subseteq \mathfrak p$, then we're done, and so assume that $\mathfrak{a}$ is not contained in $\mathfrak p$. Then there is some $a \in \mathfrak a$ such that $a \notin \mathfrak p$. But then for every $b \in \mathfrak b$, we have $ab \in \mathfrak{ab} \subseteq \mathfrak p$, and as $\mathfrak p$ is prime then $a \in \mathfrak p$ or $b \in \mathfrak p,$ so then $b \in \mathfrak p$. Thus $\mathfrak b \subseteq \mathfrak p$. So the forward direction of the equality holds. Now suppose $\mathfrak{q} \in V (\mathfrak a) \cup  V (\mathfrak b$). WLOG, suppose $\mathfrak p \supseteq \mathfrak a$. Then we have that $x = \sum _{i, j}^n a_i b_j \in \mathfrak a$ with $a_i \in \mathfrak a$ and $b_j \in \mathfrak b$, as $\mathfrak a$ is an ideal. The elements of $\mathfrak{ab}$ are precisly of the form as $x$, so $\mathfrak {ab} \subseteq \mathfrak a$, which implies $\mathfrak {ab} \subseteq \mathfrak p$. Hence the equality holds.    
(b) A prime ideal $\p \supseteq \sum \mathfrak a _i$ for a family of ideals $\\{ \mathfrak a _i\\}$ if and only if each $\mathfrak a_i$ is contained in $\p$, since $\sum \mathfrak{a}_i$ is the smallest ideal containg all of the individual ideals $\mathfrak a _i$.   
(c) Recall that for any ideal $\mathfrak I$, $\sqrt{\mathfrak I}$ is the interesection of all prime ideals that contain $\mathfrak I$. So then $\sqrt{ \mathfrak {b}} \subseteq \sqrt{\mathfrak a}$ if and only if $ V( \mathfrak a) \subseteq V( \mathfrak b)$. ▨

Furthermore, we can see clearly that $V(\varnothing) = \spec A$ and $V ((0)) = \spec A$. Now Lemma 1.2 shows that finite unions and abritrary insections of the sets $V(\mathfrak a)$ are once vanishing sets, and hence we define a topology on $\spec A$ by taking the vanishing subsets $V(\mathfrak a) \subseteq \spec A$ to be the closed subsets of our topology---this topology is called the *Zariski topology*.

**Proposition 1.1.** The closed points of $\spec A$ correspond to $V(\mathfrak m) = \\{ \mathfrak m\\}$ where $\mathfrak m$ is a maximal ideal of $A$.

*Proof.* As every maximal ideal is prime, then $\mathfrak m \in V(\mathfrak m)$ as, trivially, $\mathfrak m \subseteq \mathfrak m$. This is the only prime ideal that contains $\mathfrak m$ as, by definition, the only other ideal that contains $\mathfrak m$ is $A$, but $(1) = A$ is not a prime ideal, so $V(\mathfrak m) = \\{ \mathfrak m \\}$. Moreover, if $\p$ is a prime ideal that isn't maximal, then $\\{ \p \\}$ cannot be closed as any closed set $V(I)$ that contains $\mathfrak p$ will also contain some maximal ideal that contains $\p$. ▨

**Proposition 1.2.** If $\mathfrak a$ is an ideal, then $V(\mathfrak a) = V (\sqrt{\mathfrak a})$.

*Proof.* Clearly $\mathfrak a \subseteq \sqrt{\mathfrak a}$, then $V(\sqrt{ \mathfrak a }) \subseteq V(\mathfrak a)$ as any prime ideal containing $\sqrt{\mathfrak a}$, then the prime ideal also contains $\mathfrak a$. Now let $\p \in V(\mathfrak a)$ and take $x \in \sqrt{\mathfrak a}$. Then $x^n \in \mathfrak a$ for some $n$, which implies that $x^n \in \p$. As $\p$ is prime, a typical induction argument gives you that $x \in \p$. Thus $\sqrt{\mathfrak a } \subseteq \p$ and $\p \in V(\sqrt{\mathfrak a})$. ▨

**Corollary 1.1.** If $V(\mathfrak a ) = V(\mathfrak b)$, then $\sqrt{\mathfrak a} = \sqrt{\mathfrak b}$.

Now, for any element $f \in A$, we denote $D(f)$ to be the open complement of $V((f))$, i.e. $D(f) = \\{ \p \in \spec A \colon f \notin \p \\}$ which is to say that $D(f)$ is the locus that doesn't vanish at the point $\p$. We call $D(f)$ a *distinguished open subset* of $\spec A$

**Lemma 1.3.**    
(a) If $f \in A$, then $D(f) \sqcup V(f) = \spec A$.   
(b) If $f \in R$, then $D(f) = \varnothing$ if and only if $f$ is nilpotent.    
(c) If $f = u g$ for some unit $u \in A$, then $D(f) = D(g)$.    
(d) If $I$ is an ideal of $A$, and $\p$ is a prime ideal of $A$ with $p \notin V(I)$, then there exists an $f \in A$ such that $\p \in D(f)$, and $D(f) \cap V(I) = \varnothing$.    
(e) If $f,g \in A$, then $D(fg) = D(f) \cap D(g).$     
(f) If $f_i \in A$ for $i \in I$, then $\bigcup_{i \in I} D(f_i)$ is the complement of $V( \\{ f_i \\} _{ i \in I} )$ in $\spec A$.     
(g) If $f \in A$ and $D(f) = \spec A$, then $f$ is a unit.

*Proof.* See Lemma 10.17.2 of The Stacks Project (SP), where part (a) is (11) and (g) is (17) on SP.  ▨

## 2. Attaching a sheaf $\mathscr O$ to $\spec A$.
As we've endowed $\spec A$ with the Zariski topology, we will define a sheaf of rings $\mathscr O$ on $\spec A$. We do this by letting for each open $U \subseteq \spec A$: \\[ \mathscr{O} _X (U) = \left \\{ s \colon U \to \bigsqcup _{ \mathfrak p \in U} A _{ \mathfrak p} \colon \text{ satisfies $(\dagger)$ } \right \\} \\] where the condition $(\dagger)$ says that given $[\mathfrak p] \in U$ we have $s([\mathfrak p] ) \in A _{\mathfrak p}$ and that $s$ is locally a fraction, i.e. given $[\mathfrak p] \in U$ there is an open neighborhood $[\p] \in V \subseteq U$ and $a, f \in A$ such that for every point $[\mathfrak q] \in V$, $f \notin \mathfrak q$, and $s([\mathfrak q]) = \frac{a}{f}$. 

There is an obvious parallel to the set of all regular functions on a quasi-affine variety, however, in that scenario we have the luxury of working over an algebraically closed field where a regular function mapped from a quasi-affine variety $Y$ of an affine variety $X$ to the base field $k$, but with working over a ring $A$, there is a chances that $s([\mathfrak p])$ wouldn't be mapped into a consistent underlying space, so we have to consider function into the various local rings. 

**Proposition 2.1.1.** Let $A$ be a ring, and $(\spec, \mathscr O)$ its spectrum.   
(a) For any $\mathfrak p \in \spec A$, the stalk $\mathscr O _{\mathfrak p}$ of the sheaf $\mathscr O$ is isomorphic to the local ring $A _{ \p}$.    
(b) For any element $ f \in A$, the ring $\mathscr O (D(f)) \simeq A_f$.     
(c) In particular, $\Gamma (\spec A, \mathscr O) = \mathscr O (\spec A) \simeq A$.

*Proof.* (a) We need to firstly define a map $\mathscr O _{\mathfrak p} \to A _{\mathfrak p}$: take some $[(U, s)] \in \mathscr O _{\mathfrak p}$, which means that we have $s \in \mathscr O_X (U)$ and $\mathfrak p \in U$, and so $s \colon U \to \bigsqcup _{[\mathfrak q] \in U } A _{\mathfrak q}$. To get an element in $A _{ \mathfrak p}$ given $s$, its natural to consider the image of $\mathfrak p$ under $s$, i.e. $s(\mathfrak p) \in A _{\mathfrak p}$. Now defining $s \mapsto s( \mathfrak p)$ gives our desired map from $\mathscr O _{\mathfrak p}$ to $A _{\mathfrak p}$. To show that the map is well defined is easy. Suppose $[(U, s)]= [(V, s)]$ in $\mathscr O _{\mathfrak p}$, which is to say that the two germs agree at their restriction at a common open set containing the point $[\mathfrak p]$, so there is an open $W \subseteq U \cap V$ such that $s \mid _W = t \mid _V$. As $\mathfrak p \in W$, then $s (\mathfrak p) = t (\mathfrak p)$, and so the map $\mathscr O _{\mathfrak p} \to A _{\mathfrak p}$ is well defined. Now suppose that we have $[(U, s)], [(V,  t)] \in \mathscr O _{\mathfrak p}$ such that $s(\p) = t(\p)$. As $[\p] \in U$, for $s \colon U \to \bigsqcup _{\mathfrak q \in U} A _{\mathfrak q}$ there is some open neighborhood $ [\p] \in U _{ [\p ]} \subseteq U$ and $a, f \in A$ such that $\frac{a}{f}$, $f \notin \p$, and for all $\mathfrak q \in U _{\p}$, we have $s (\mathfrak q) = \frac{a}{f}$. In a similar fashion, we have $t \colon V \to \bigsqcup _{\mathfrak q \in V} A _{\mathfrak q}$ with some open neighborhood $\mathfrak p \in V _{\p } \subseteq V$ and $b, g \in A$, $g \notin \mathfrak q$, with $t(\mathfrak q) = \frac{b}{g}$ for all $\mathfrak q \in V _{\mathfrak p}$. So our inital assumption implies that $s(\p ) = \frac{a}{f} = \frac{b}{g} = t( \p)$. Let $\mathfrak q \in U _{\p} \cap V _{\p}$. Then $ s (\mathfrak q) = \frac{a}{f} = \frac{b}{g} = t(\mathfrak q)$, which means that $s \mid _{ U _{\p} \cap V _{\p}} = t \mid _{ U _{\p } \cap V _{\p}}$, so $[(U, s)] = [(V, t)]$. Thus $s \mapsto s(\p)$ is injective. Lastly, let $\frac{a}{f} \in A _{\mathfrak p}$ with $a \in A$ and $f \in A \setminus \p$. We want to pick an open set $U$ containg $\mathfrak p$ and $s \colon U \to \bigsqcup _{\mathfrak q \in U} A _{\mathfrak q}$ such that $s(\mathfrak p) = \frac{a}{f}$. Suppose $f \notin \mathfrak q$, that is, $\mathfrak q \in D(f)$. Let $U = D(f)$ and consider $s \colon U \to \bigsqcup _{\mathfrak q \in U} A _{\mathfrak q}$ which sends $\mathfrak q$ to image of $\frac{a}{f}$ in $A _{\mathfrak q}$. Thus we have $s( \p )= \frac{a}{f} \in A _{\mathfrak q}$, and so the map is surjective. Hence we have an isomorphism $\mathscr O _{\mathfrak p} \to A _{\mathfrak p}$, where $s \mapsto s(\mathfrak p)$.   
(b) Take $f \in A$. We want to define a map $A _f \to \mathscr O (D(f))$ and produce a bijection. Firstly, we define the map $\psi \colon A_f \to \mathscr O (D(f))$ by sending $\frac{a}{f^n}$ to the section $s \in \mathscr O (D(f))$ which assigns to each $\mathfrak p$ the image of $\frac{a}{f^n} \in A _{\p}$. For injectivity, suppose $\psi (\frac{a}{f^n}) = \psi (\frac{b}{f^m})$, then for every $\mathfrak p \in D(f)$, we have that $\frac{a}{f^n}$ and $\frac{b}{f^m}$ have the same image in $A _{\p}$. So then by definition, there is an $h \in A \setminus \p$ such that $h(af^m - bf^n) =0$ in $A$. Now let $\mathfrak A$ denote the annihilator of $f^m a - f^n b$. Then $h \in \mathfrak A$, but $h \notin \p$, so we can clearly see that $\mathfrak A \nsubseteq \p$. This translates to: for any $\p \in D(f)$ we have $V(\mathfrak A ) \cap D(f) = \varnothing$. Thus $f \in \sqrt{ \mathfrak A}$ as $V(\mathfrak A) \cap D (f) = \varnothing$ is equivalent to saying $V(\mathfrak A) \subseteq V(f)$, so $\sqrt{(f)} \subseteq \sqrt{\mathfrak A}$. So then we have $f\in \mathfrak A$ such that $f^k (f^m a -f^n) = 0$ so then $\frac{a}{f^n} = \frac{b}{f^m}$. Hence $\psi $ is injective. Now we have to show the map $\psi$ is surjective, which is long and more difficult. For now, I refer the rest of the proof to Hartshorne II.2.2.(b).   
(c) This is simply the special case of (b) where $f =1$, and $D(f) = \spec A$. ▨

**Definition 2.1.2.** A **ringed space** is a pair $(X, \mathscr O _X)$ consisting of a topological space $X$ and a sheaf of rings $\mathscr O _X$ on $X$. A **morphism** of ringed space $(X, \mathscr O _X) \to (Y, \mathscr O _Y)$ is a pair $(f, f ^{\sharp})$ of a continuous map $f \colon X \to Y$ and a map $f ^{\sharp} \colon \mathscr O _Y \to f _{\ast} \mathscr O _X$ of sheaves of rings on $Y$, where $(f _{\ast} \mathscr O_X):= \mathscr O _X (f^{-1} (V))$ for any open set $V \subseteq Y$, and the map $f ^{\sharp}$ is called the *pullback*. The ringed space $(X, \mathscr O _X)$ is a **locally ringed space** if for each point $P \in X$, the stalk $\mathscr O _{X, P}$ is a local ring. A **morphism** of locally ringed spaces is a morphism $(f, f ^{\sharp})$ of ringed spaces, such that for each point $P \in X$, the induced map of local rings $f ^{\sharp} _P \colon \mathscr O _{Y, f(P)} \to  \mathscr O _{X, P}$ is a **local homomorphism** of local rings. Recall that a local homomorphism is defined by: If $A$ and $B$ are local rings with maximal ideals $\mathfrak m _A$ and $\mathfrak m _B$ respectively, a homomorphism $\varphi \colon A \to B$ is called a *local homomorphism* if $\varphi ^{-1} (\mathfrak m _B) = \mathfrak m _A$. An **isomorphism** of locally ringed spaces is a morphism with a two-sided inverse. Thus a morphism $(f, f ^{\sharp})$ is an isomorphism if and only if $f$ is a homeomorphism of the underlying topological spaces, and $f^ {\sharp}$ is an isomorphism of sheaves.

## 3. Schemes
Now we proceed to actually define what object we have an interest in, which are, in a way, affine varieties for commutative rings---ref. [*Varieties as Schemes*](https://math.berkeley.edu/~mhaiman/math256-fall18-spring19/varieties-as-schemes-v2.pdf) by Mark Haiman, where the main theorem established is the following: 

**Theorem.** Let $k$ be an algebraically closed field. The category of reduced algebraic $k$-schemes is equivalent to the category of classical algebraic varieties over $k$

**Definition 2.2.4.** An **affine scheme** is a locally ringed space $(X, \mathscr O _X)$ which is isomorphic (as a locally ringed space) to the spectrum of some ring. A **scheme** is a locally ringed space $(X, \mathscr O _X)$ that has an open cover by affine schemes. We call $X$ the *underlying topological space* of the scheme $(X, \mathscr O _X)$, and $\mathscr O _X$ its *structure sheaf*. Typically we write just $X$ to denote the scheme $(X, \mathscr O _X)$. A **morphism** of schemes is a morphism of locally ringed spaces. An **isomorphism** is a morphism with a two-sided inverse. 

**Remark 2.2.5.** In some literature, one says that a *scheme* $(X, \mathscr O _X)$ is a locally ringed space in which every point of $X$ has an open neighborhood $U$ such that the topological space $U$, together with the *restricted sheaf* $\mathscr O _{X \mid _U }$ is an affine scheme. The restriction sheaf is given in a natural way: if $U$ is open in $X$, then we define a restricted sheaf $\mathscr O _{X \mid _U}$, where for every open $V \subseteq U$, we set $\mathscr O _{X \mid _U}(V) = \mathscr O _{X} (V)$.

## 3.1. Categorical Relations between $\mathfrak{AffSch}$ and $\mathfrak{Rings}$. 

Affine schemes form a category $\mathfrak{AffSch}$, which is a subcategory of the category of locally ringed spaces. The work we did for Lemma 2.2.2. gave us a hint of the similarity between rings and affine schemes: the assignment $A \mapsto \spec A$ gives us a contravariant functor from the category $\mathfrak{Rings}$ to the category $\mathfrak{AffSch}$. Similarily, Corollary 2.2.3. gave us the other contravariant functor in the opposite direction: taking the global sections of the structure sheaf $\mathscr O _{\spec A}$ gives us the ring $A$ back. Moreover a map of affine schemes $f \colon \spec B \to \spec A$ is associated with a map of sheaves $f ^{\sharp} \colon \mathscr O _{\spec A} \to f _{\ast} \mathscr O _{\spec B}$. After taking global sections, \\[A = \Gamma (\spec A, \mathscr O _{\spec A}) \to \Gamma (\spec A, f _{\ast} \mathscr O _{\spec B}) = B .\\] Now we naturally define a canonical map \\[ \Gamma \colon \Hom _{\mathfrak{AffSch}} (X, Y) \to \Hom _{\mathfrak{Rings}} (\mathscr O _Y (Y), \mathscr O _X (X))  \\] where $(f, f ^{\sharp})$ is sent to the map $f ^{\sharp} (Y)\colon \mathscr O _Y (Y) \to \mathscr O _X (X)$.

**Proposition 3.2.1.** If $X$ and $Y$ are affine schemes, then the map $\Gamma$ is bijective.

From this discussion, we see that affine schemes $X$ are completely determined by their corresponding rings of global sections $\Gamma (X, \mathscr O _X)$, and morphisms between affine schemes $X \to Y$ are in $1 \colon 1$ correspondence with ring homomorphisms $\Gamma (Y, \mathscr O _Y) \to \Gamma (X, \mathscr O _X)$. This establishes that a map $f$ between two affine schemes is an isomorphism if and only if the corresponding map of rings $f ^ {\sharp}$ is an isomorphism.

We should note here however that there is a stronger statement of Theorem 3.2.2 where we consider schemes in general rather than affine schemes:

**Theorem 3.2.3.** Let $X$ be an a scheme, and let $A$ be a ring. The canonical map \\[ \Hom _{\mathfrak{Sch}} (X, \spec A) \to \Hom _{\mathfrak{Rings}} (A, \Gamma (X, \mathscr O _X)) \\]given $(f, f ^{\sharp}) \mapsto f ^{\sharp} (X)$ is bijective.

**Remark 3.2.1.** The schemes over $S$ form a category $\mathfrak{Sch} /S$, and the set of morphisms as described above is denoted $\Hom _{S} (X, Y)$. To reiterate, the schemes over $S$ are morphisms of schemes $f \colon X \to S$, also called *relative schemes* over $S$ or $S$-schemes*. A morphism $h \colon (f \colon X \to S) \to (g \colon Y \to S)$ is a morphism $h \colon X \to Y$ of schemes such that $g \circ h = f$.     
**Remark 3.2.2.** Ever scheme $Y$ is a scheme over $\spec \zz$. We can write $ Y = \bigcup _i U_i$ where $U_i \simeq \spec A_i$ for ring $A_i$. Then as $\zz$ is the inital object in the category of rings, then there is a canonical homomorphism $\varphi _i \colon \zz \to A_i$ which induces maps $\spec A_i \to \spec \zz$. Composing these maps with the established isomorphisms $U_i \simeq \spec A_i$, we get a map $Y = \bigcup U_i \to \spec \zz$. On the level of categories, this says that $\spec \zz$ is the terminal object in $\mathfrak{Sch}$, i.e. every scheme is a $\zz$-scheme, and furthermore that $\mathfrak{Sch} = \mathfrak{Sch} / \zz$.

Recall that if $B$ is an algebra over a ring $A$, then we say that $B$ is *finitely presented* if it is the quotient of a polynomial ring $A[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$ over $A$ by a finitely generated ideal. If $A$ is noetherian, every finitely generated algebra is finitely presented. Moreover, if $B$ is finitely presented over $A$ whenever we write $B = A[x_1, \ldots, x_n] / I$, the ideal $I$ is always finitely generated in $A[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$. Now, an algebra $B$ over $A$ is called *finite* if $B$ is a finitely generated $A$-module, which is to say that there elements $b_1, \ldots, b_n \in B$ such that $B = Ab_1 + \cdots + A b_n$. An algebra $B$ over $A$ is said to be *finitely generated* (or of *finite type*) if $B$ is a finitely generated $A$-algebra, i.e. there are elements $b_1, \ldots, b_n$ such that $B = A [b_1, \ldots, b_n]$. We can remark here that an algebra $A \to B$ is finite if and only if it is of finite type and integral (e.g. $\zz \to \zz [\sqrt{2}]$ is of finite type over $\zz$ and integral, so indeed finite).

**Definition 3.2.3.** Let $X/S$ be a scheme over $S$ with structure morphism $f \colon X \to S$. One says that  
i) $X/S$ is of **locally finite type** if $S$ has a cover consisting of open affine subsets $V_i = \spec A_i$ such that each $f ^{-1} (V_i)$ can be covered by affine subsets of the form $\spec B _{ij}$, where each $B _{ij}$ is finitely generated as an $A_i$-algebra;   
ii) $f$ is of **finite type** if, in (i), one can do with a finite number of $\spec B _{ij}$.  

In the special case where $S$ is affine, say, $S = \spec T$, one says that a scheme $X$ over $S$ is of *locally finite type* (resp. *finite type*) over $S$ if the structure morphism $X \to \spec T$ is locally of finite type (resp. finite type). Speaking of special cases, our notion of a scheme being of locally finite type/of finite type is a weaker restriction of a stronger finiteness property a morphism can have:

**Definition 3.2.4.** Let $f \colon X \to S$ be a scheme over $S$. We say that    
i) $f$ is **affine** if there is a covering $\spec A_i$ of $S$ such that each $f ^{-1} (\spec A_i)$ is itself affine;   
ii) $f$ is **finite** if it is affine, and in the notation above, if for each $f ^{-1} (\spec A_i) = \spec B_i$, the $A_i$-algebra $B_i$ is a finitely generated $A_i$ module. 

**Proposition 3.2.5.** Let $X/S$ be a scheme over $S$ with structure morphism $f \colon X \to S$. Then $X/S$ is of locally finite type (resp. finite type / resp. affine / resp. finite) if $X/S$ is of locally finite type (resp. finite type / resp. affine/ resp. finite) for one affine covering of $S$. 

## 3.3 Open and Closed Immersions & Open and Closed subschemes

Let $X$ be a scheme and $U \subseteq X$ be an open subset of the underlying topological space. Then the restriction sheaf $\mathscr O _{X \mid U}$ is a sheaf on $U$ making $(U, \mathscr O _{ X \mid U})$ into a locally ringed space. Furthmore, $(U, \mathscr O _{X \mid U})$ is scheme since $X$ covered by affines $\spec A _i$, then each $U \cap \spec A_i$ is open in $\spec A_i$, and so it can be covered by affine schemes.
